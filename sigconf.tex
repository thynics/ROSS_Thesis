\begin{abstract}
Earliest-finish-time scheduling accounts for communication that delays the current task, yet a favorable read--write placement can move the unique valid version of an object and induce larger successor traffic. We formalize this HEFT--MSI conflict and introduce \textbf{prefix-safe copy-aware HEFT}, which accepts a lower-copy alternative only if it preserves the modeled finish of committed placement decisions, up to a \(10^{-6}\) ms tolerance. It predicts no unscheduled suffix. A narrow \textbf{successor-aware locality certification (SALC)} companion evaluates a modeled one-hop risk for a graph-visible, fixed-device successor.

We evaluate the method by separating observations with an actionable HEFT--MSI conflict from weak- or no-opportunity controls, and by using exact gate removal to expose over-optimization. A previously qualified strong-opportunity block contains two problem instances on one 8-GPU NVIDIA L4 node. Across 30 provenance- and correctness-checked executions, prefix-safe outperforms HEFT in all five Cholesky pairs (1.315x geomean, 81.1\% modeled-copy reduction) and all five LU pairs (1.171x, 39.4\%). Gate removal helps Cholesky by 1.162x but loses to prefix-safe on LU by 1.205x despite removing 95.7\% more modeled copy. This supports a condition-dependent safety boundary, not a benchmark-specific heuristic or a universal speedup source. In a controlled CG case, one fixed-successor SALC intervention conditionally eliminates seven attributed peer-copy requests and is followed by 27 downstream placement changes. Every group, including HEFT, misses our predeclared 5\% scheduler-share target; this is a prototype limitation, not incremental prefix-gate overhead.

\section{Introduction}\label{introduction}

Online heterogeneous schedulers must decide where to execute a task before the rest of a dynamic graph has necessarily become runnable. HEFT-style scheduling makes this decision by estimating each device's earliest finish time (EFT), including the time needed to make the current task's inputs available. This is a strong local objective: it combines device availability, current input readiness, and task duration in a single comparable quantity.

The local objective is nevertheless incomplete when placement changes data state. In CUDASTF, a read--write access leaves the new version Modified on the selected GPU and invalidates stale copies. A placement that wins a fraction of a millisecond for the current task can consequently move a large mutable version away from the GPU where a later task must consume it. The later task either copies the version back or follows it and moves other inputs. In both cases the locally best EFT decision can initiate a residency cascade whose traffic is larger than the immediate gain.

This paper asks a general systems question: can an online scheduler exploit residency-aware copy savings without paying arbitrary delay in the work already committed, and remain safe when that opportunity is absent? Our answer is a prefix-bounded refinement. We first retain HEFT's device as a baseline. We then consider devices that reduce modeled current-input copy and admit one only if its finish fits the baseline's current-prefix finish bound up to numerical tolerance. The rule is benchmark-independent: it applies to ordinary tasks and does not require a special mutable-chain label. Its benefit is necessarily condition-dependent because some graphs expose no actionable HEFT--MSI conflict. It also remains intentionally local. The safety condition says nothing about the makespan of the unscheduled suffix.

We separately ask whether the motivating migrate-then-return pattern can ever be identified directly. For a controlled case with an explicit fixed-device successor, SALC compares a one-hop avoided output-copy lower bound against the producer's HEFT-relative EFT damage. This companion identifies the constructed chain, but its evidence is a controlled mechanism certificate rather than natural-CG performance.

Our contributions are:

\begin{enumerate}
\def\labelenumi{\arabic{enumi}.}
\tightlist
\item
  We give an executable-system formalization showing that HEFT's current-task EFT objective and MSI residency transitions admit a legal migrate-then-return counterexample.
\item
  We define a translation-invariant, prefix-safe copy-aware refinement that preserves the modeled finish bound of the current scheduling prefix up to numerical tolerance while optimizing a secondary copy-and-queue score.
\item
  We implement a conservative fixed-successor SALC certificate and validate one intervention-consistent CG-derived trace using placement, data identity, correctness, and runtime-issued transfer evidence.
\item
  We define a problem-oriented evaluation that separates artifact validity, modeled-copy opportunity, latency response, and gate-removal risk. A qualified 30-run gate-only block supplies two strong-opportunity observations with opposite ablation outcomes, together with an explicit negative scheduler-share result and coverage boundary.
\end{enumerate}

The resulting claim is deliberately smaller than ``automatic chain discovery'' but broader than a policy for two named benchmarks. Prefix safety is a general admission rule for the HEFT--MSI conflict; fixed-successor SALC certifies one graph-visible edge pattern. Neither method proves a suffix or global makespan improvement, and the generality of the rule does not imply universal speedup when the motivating conflict is absent.

\section{System Model and HEFT--MSI Conflict}\label{system-model-and-heftmsi-conflict}

\subsection{Online task and residency model}\label{online-task-and-residency-model}

Let \(G=(V,E)\) be a task DAG and \(D=\{0,\ldots,m-1\}\) the GPU set. Task \(v\) has predicted compute time \(p_v>0\). A logical data object \(x\) has modeled copy time

\[
c_x = \frac{\operatorname{bytes}(x)}{B},
\]

where \(B\) is the scheduler's explicit copy-bandwidth parameter. At scheduling step \(k\), \(L_k(d)\) is GPU \(d\)'s modeled ready time and \(M_k(x,d)\in\{M,S,I\}\) is the scheduler's Modified/Shared/Invalid residency state.

For current task \(v\), let \(A_k(v,d)\) be the latest availability time among its read and read--write inputs on \(d\). A local Modified or Shared instance incurs no transfer; otherwise availability includes a copy from a valid instance. CUDASTF's current HEFT estimate is

\[
F_k(v,d) = \max\{L_k(d), A_k(v,d)\} + p_v,
\qquad
b = \arg\min_{d\in D} F_k(v,d),
\]

with a deterministic tie order. We call \(b\) the \textbf{HEFT baseline device}. After a write-like access on \(d\), the scheduler's MSI transition makes the new version valid at \(d\) and invalidates conflicting copies elsewhere.

Here ``HEFT'' denotes this CUDASTF fork's HEFT-style current-task device selector; the runtime supplies task order rather than recomputing the offline upward-rank order of canonical HEFT. Equal-EFT candidates are resolved by lower device ID.

This formulation also explains why the earlier copy-gain-plus-penalty idea can work even though HEFT already models communication. HEFT folds current input readiness into a \textbf{maximum} before adding compute time, whereas the secondary copy quantity \(K(d)\) below \textbf{sums} modeled transfers. A busy device or one late dependency can dominate \(F_k\) and hide several other transfers: two devices can have the same current-prefix finish while moving very different total bytes. Copy gain exposes that hidden locality slack; the prefix gate prevents the scheduler from buying lower traffic by extending the finish frontier that HEFT has already established. The method is therefore a secondary refinement of HEFT, not a claim that HEFT ignores communication.

The transfer quantities reported in this paper require precise language. \textbf{Modeled copy bytes/time} are computed by the scheduler's MSI and bandwidth model. \textbf{Runtime-issued GPU-peer transfers} are data-interface copies that CUDASTF actually requests between distinct GPU endpoints. They are not CUPTI transactions, link utilization, or physical PCIe/NVLink bus traffic.

\subsection{Conflict definition}\label{conflict-definition}

Let \(\Phi_k(v,d)\) denote the future data-movement penalty induced by placing \(v\) on \(d\). This is a conceptual suffix quantity, not something our main method computes. A HEFT--MSI locality conflict exists for devices \(g,h\) when

\[
F_k(v,g) < F_k(v,h)
\]

but

\[
\Phi_k(v,g)-\Phi_k(v,h)
  > F_k(v,h)-F_k(v,g).
\]

Thus the ordering of \(g\) and \(h\) is reversed under \(F_k+\Phi_k\). When \(D=\{g,h\}\), as in the construction below, their global minimizers differ; with more devices, the definition asserts only this pairwise reversal.

A two-GPU construction establishes existence. Let a mutable object \(x\) be valid on GPU 1 and invalid on GPU 0, with copy time \(C\). Set GPU loads to \(L(0)=0\) and \(L(1)=C+\epsilon\), where \(0<\epsilon<C\). A read--write task \(v\) finishes at \(C+p_v\) on GPU 0 and at \(C+\epsilon+p_v\) on GPU 1, so HEFT moves \(x\) to GPU 0 for an \(\epsilon\) advantage. Now give successor \(u\) another large input that fixes its preferred execution at GPU 1. Under HEFT, \(u\) copies the new version of \(x\) back at cost \(C\); if \(v\) had stayed on GPU 1, that copy would be absent. Thus the construction proves a \(C\)-byte traffic difference. If \(u\) lies on the serial dependency path, no intervening task overwrites \(x\), and the return copy is not completely hidden by other waiting, the suffix completion additionally loses \(C-\epsilon>0\).

This is an existential statement. It does not imply that HEFT usually fails, that every read--write task belongs to a harmful chain, or that any local correction improves the full DAG.

\section{Bounded Locality Refinements}\label{bounded-locality-refinements}

\subsection{Prefix-safe copy-aware HEFT}\label{prefix-safe-copy-aware-heft}

For the current task, let \(K(d)\) be the sum of modeled transfer times for its read and read--write inputs on device \(d\). Let \(T(d)=\max\{L_k(d),A_k(v,d)\}\) and define the modeled queue delay

\[
Q(d)=\max\{0,T(d)-A_k(v,d)\}.
\]

We first compute the HEFT baseline \(b\), breaking equal EFTs by lower device ID. Let

\[
R = \max\left\{\text{current system finish},\; \max_{d\in D} L_k(d)\right\}
\]

be the current ready-time frontier, and define the baseline and candidate prefix finishes

\[
P_b = \max\{R,F(b)\}, \qquad P(d)=\max\{R,F(d)\}.
\]

The prefix gate admits \(d\ne b\) only if

\[
P(d) \le P_b + \varepsilon_p.
\]

An alternative may therefore have a later per-task EFT than HEFT when that difference is hidden under the already established ready frontier, but it may increase the modeled finish of the current scheduling prefix by at most the numerical tolerance \(\varepsilon_p\). Adding the same constant to every ready and finish time changes neither this decision nor the queue-delay terms, avoiding a sensitivity of gates normalized by absolute EFT.

\textbf{Modeled current-prefix invariant.} If candidate \(d\) passes the gate above, replacing \(b\) by \(d\) increases the scheduler's current prefix-finish quantity by at most \(\varepsilon_p\). This is the acceptance predicate itself: \(P(d)\le P_b+\varepsilon_p\). Adding the same constant to every ready and finish time leaves both sides unchanged, so the predicate is time-shift invariant. The implementation fixes \(\varepsilon_p=10^{-6}\) ms. The invariant concerns the model's scalar \(\max\{R,F\}\); it does not preserve the current task's EFT, the unscheduled suffix, or observed wall time. Here ``prefix'' means placement decisions already committed, not a predicted critical-path prefix.

Among prefix-safe alternatives, we retain the existing copy-aware selectivity. Define

\[
G_K(d)=\frac{K(b)-K(d)}{\max\{K_{\min},K(b)\}},
\qquad
G_Q(d)=\frac{Q(b)-Q(d)}{\max\{Q_{\min},Q(b)\}}.
\]

The candidate must have a strictly positive copy saving and satisfy \(G_K(d)\ge\tau\). Let \(n_v\) be dependency count, \(B_v\) the bytes of read/read--write inputs, \(p_v\) the predicted task time in ms, and \(\operatorname{clip}(z)=\min\{1,\max\{0,z\}\}\). Define

\[
\begin{aligned}
c_p&=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{\ln(1+p_v)}{\ln 501}\right),
&c_n&=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{n_v}{16}\right),\\
c_B&=\operatorname{clip}\!\left(\frac{\ln(1+B_v)}{\ln(1+2^{30})}\right),
&c(v)&=\operatorname{clip}(0.55c_p+0.30c_n+0.15c_B).
\end{aligned}
\]

In the frozen configuration, \(\tau=0.25\) when \(c(v)>0.60\) and \(0.15\) otherwise, and \(\lambda_Q=0.35\). These inherited values are shared by every benchmark, were frozen before the repeated confirmation, and are not claimed optimal. Eligible alternatives are ranked by

\[
S(d)=G_K(d)+\lambda_Q\max\{0,G_Q(d)\},
\]

then by lower EFT and lower device ID under a score tie. The selected device is HEFT when no candidate passes both gates.

\begin{Verbatim}[fontsize=\scriptsize]
PREFIX-SAFE-COPY(task v, candidates D)
    b <- canonical HEFT candidate
    R <- max(current system finish, max device ready time)
    P_b <- max(R, F(b))
    eligible <- empty
    for d in D, d != b:
        if candidate fields are not finite: continue
        copy_saving <- K(b) - K(d)
        copy_gain <- copy_saving / max(K_min, K(b))
        if copy_saving <= eps or copy_gain < tau(v): continue
        if F(d) > P_b + eps_prefix: continue
        queue_gain <- (Q(b) - Q(d)) / max(Q_min, Q(b))
        score <- copy_gain + lambda_Q * max(0, queue_gain)
        add (d, score) to eligible
    if eligible is empty: return b
    return highest score, then lower EFT, then lower device ID
\end{Verbatim}

The method records accepted copy saving, HEFT-relative EFT damage, and prefix damage. Verification requires aggregate prefix damage to be zero within numerical tolerance. The broad mode is our main method. A one-read--write/unique-owner restriction was also implemented, but every task in the studied Cholesky and LU DAGs satisfied that scope, and the restricted and broad variants produced identical placements. The scope is therefore not evidence of chain detection.

For controlled mechanism isolation, the source-matched \texttt{no-prefix} ablation retains the same canonical HEFT baseline, candidate set, copy and queue gains, thresholds, score, and tie breaks, but disables only the prefix admission rule. It consequently has no Proposition 1 guarantee. This exact removal distinguishes the gate's effect from the earlier conventional relative-EFT comparator, which changes the timing rule itself.

\subsection{Fixed-successor SALC companion}\label{fixed-successor-salc-companion}

Prefix safety cannot accept a device whose current EFT extends \(P_b\), even when one known future copy is much larger. SALC addresses only a graph-visible version of that case.

For producer \(v\), SALC identifies write-like output symbols read by direct successors. If successor \(u\) is explicitly fixed to GPU \(a(u)\), that GPU is an authoritative anchor. For a hypothetical producer device \(d\), a conservative one-hop modeled copy quantity is

\[
\Phi_1(d)=
\sum_{(x,a)\in U}
\mathbf{1}[d\ne a]\frac{\operatorname{bytes}(x)}{B},
\]

where \(U\) deduplicates output-symbol/anchor pairs. For HEFT baseline \(b\) and alternative \(d\), SALC computes

\[
G_{\mathrm{future}}(d)=\Phi_1(b)-\Phi_1(d),
\qquad
\Delta_F(d)=\max\{0,F(d)-F(b)\},
\]

and accepts only when

\[
C_{\mathrm{SALC}}(d)
=G_{\mathrm{future}}(d)-\Delta_F(d)
> \mu,
\]

for margin \(\mu\) (zero in the controlled certificate). SALC selects the positive candidate with the largest certificate, breaking ties by EFT.

The inequality is a certificate only when the producer creates the unique relevant version, the fixed successor reads that same version, and no intervening task overwrites it. It certifies a modeled one-hop edge risk, not actual saved link time, critical-path delay, suffix speedup, or global makespan. Although the implementation can form a shadow anchor for non-fixed successors, the positive evidence here is specifically the authoritative fixed-successor case.

\section{Controlled CG-Derived Chain Certificate}\label{controlled-cg-derived-chain-certificate}

A direct \texttt{cg\_dot} successor is fixed to GPU 0, so task 48 returns the produced data; five later fixed reads repeat that direction.

Our primary problem-boundary protocol is a separate current-source block over FDTD, standard CG, Cholesky, LU, and MiniWeather. This structural exception handles repeatable same-GPU contention tails on launch-bound scalar kernels while preserving a two-thirds central majority and the independent MAD gate. The rule was fixed from raw calibration failures before any expanded-block performance result was read; raw samples, selected floors, inlier counts, and pass/fail decisions remain archived.

\subsection{Metrics and statistics}\label{metrics-and-statistics}

We report the benchmark interval, paired HEFT-relative speedup, and scheduler-modeled input-copy bytes. For the five clean pairs we report the geomean, all observed directions, and the descriptive minimum--maximum range; with only five fixed-order pairs we do not claim a population confidence interval. Copy bytes/time are model outputs, not physical-link measurements.

For the expanded problem-boundary analysis, we freeze the interpretation before reading the unified results: a paired geomean speedup of at least 1.03x is a material improvement, at least 0.97x but below 1.03x is neutral/non-degrading, and below 0.97x is a regression that must be reported. Problem opportunity is an independent mechanism signal: a positive reduction in scheduler-modeled copy bytes relative to HEFT. A latency gain without that signal is reported but is not counted as evidence for the copy-aware mechanism; a weak/no-opportunity observation within the neutral band supports non-degradation rather than speedup.

We classify the prefix gate itself with a direct paired P--N comparison, independently of P--H opportunity. P materially protects against gate removal when the geomean of \(T_N/T_P\) is at least 1.03; N exposes a material conservative cost when the geomean of \(T_P/T_N\) is at least 1.03; otherwise the gate effect is neutral. A materially better P together with greater N copy reduction or positive N prefix damage is an over-optimization counterexample. A materially better N together with greater copy reduction is reported as conservative gate cost rather than suppressed as a failed benchmark.

Scheduler share is the sum of measured per-task scheduler decision time divided by the benchmark interval. It is not automatically critical-path slowdown. Our predeclared 5\% target is an engineering target for this prototype, not a community deployability standard. Absolute scheduler milliseconds and microseconds per task are reported alongside the ratio, and HEFT is evaluated by the same definition.

\subsection{Control validity}\label{fail-closed-identity-boundary}

An older broad experiment used a zero-weight same-path control. It reproduced HEFT exactly for Cholesky, LU, and MiniWeather but failed identity for standard CG in all five repeats; we therefore report no standard-CG HEFT-relative performance from that historical block. A later one-run repair cannot retroactively qualify the frozen block, but neither failure excludes independently qualified direct H--P--N observations. Separately executed identity-control rows also differ from HEFT by roughly \(-0.3\%\) to \(+3.4\%\) in one run despite identical placement, demonstrating why small one-run timing differences are not interpreted.

\subsection{Scheduler cost and excluded evidence}\label{negative-findings-determine-the-verdict}

Every clean group misses our predeclared 5\% scheduler-share target in all five repeats. Median shares for H, P, and N are respectively 13.47\%, 13.10\%, and 15.40\% on Cholesky; on LU they are 10.87\%, 10.11\%, and 8.63\%. Summed scheduler times for H, P, and N are respectively 2485.8, 1832.3, and 1854.0 ms on Cholesky, and 2513.4, 1992.9, and 2054.4 ms on LU. The corresponding per-task costs are 10.337, 7.620, and 7.710 us on Cholesky, and 9.527, 7.554, and 7.787 us on LU. Prefix-safe is cheaper than HEFT in absolute scheduler time and per-task cost on both workloads, but its shorter benchmark interval still leaves the share above target. Thus the result is not incremental overhead caused by the prefix gate: the prototype scheduler path as a whole misses the target, and these measurements do not localize the cause.

An older source snapshot supplies secondary baseline and neutral-MiniWeather context, while a one-run compatibility bridge verifies deterministic comparator semantics. Their calibration and binaries differ from the clean experiment, so none of their timings are pooled or used as headline performance. Standard-CG performance from the historical same-path block is excluded because its identity control failed. We also exclude a dense-DAG transfer diagnostic whose raw logs were not archived and a MiniWeather SALC diagnostic without an emitted executed SHA.

Finally, a one-read--write/unique-owner restriction covers every task in the two dense observations and produces the same placements as the broad method. It therefore does not identify chains; prefix-safe remains a general locality refinement rather than a dense-DAG special case.

\section{Limitations and Threats to Validity}\label{limitations-and-threats-to-validity}

\textbf{Guarantee.} Prefix safety constrains only the modeled finish of placement decisions committed so far. SALC certifies one fixed-successor modeled inequality under a same-version/no-overwrite condition. Neither mechanism bounds an unscheduled suffix, proves global makespan improvement, or identifies a critical path.

\textbf{Problem coverage, selection, and arrival model.} The admission rule is defined independently of workload identity, but the current strongest repeated evidence comes from two locality-sensitive configurations chosen after one-run exploration. These observations establish behavior when the conflict is present; they do not estimate its prevalence across applications. Weak/no-opportunity observations are needed to test non-degradation, and negative results there remain part of the method boundary rather than failed attempts to win every benchmark. The repeated DAGs are fully materialized before deferred submission. Decisions are still made one task at a time, but the evaluation does not validate dynamic task arrival, and the positive SALC case relies on a graph-visible fixed successor. Results come from one 8-L4 node, one size per workload, and five repeats.

\textbf{Statistics and baselines.} The fixed H--P--N order can correlate direction with machine drift; 5/5 consistency and the exact gate-only code delta are descriptive controlled evidence, not a randomized causal proof. The repeated evidence lacks a strong repeated weighted-copy or data-affinity baseline. We therefore claim improvement only over this runtime's HEFT baseline and use the no-prefix comparison to isolate the gate.

\textbf{Copy and memory model.} One 20.7 GB/s scalar and GPU-0 task-cost calibration are shared across identical L4s. The model omits pairwise NODE/SYS bandwidth differences, contention, overlap, memory capacity, and eviction. Modeled bytes/time are not physical PCIe traffic, and the results do not cover oversubscription or out-of-core execution.

\textbf{Traffic attribution.} Runtime-issued transfer accounting validates CUDASTF copy requests, not bus transactions. The controlled CG trace has no independent runtime version epoch, so its seven attributed requests are conditional on the task/data/fixed-successor join. CUPTI or equivalent profiling would be required for physical-link byte or time claims.

\textbf{Scheduler cost.} All six groups miss our predeclared share target even though prefix-safe's absolute scheduler cost is lower than HEFT's. The measurements do not isolate whether candidate evaluation, residency bookkeeping, or other scheduler work dominates; lower-overhead implementation and broader validation remain future work.

\section{Related Work}\label{related-work}

\textbf{HEFT and bounded lookahead.} HEFT ranks a heterogeneous DAG and selects the processor that minimizes the current task's EFT, including communication that affects current input readiness \citep{topcuoglu2002heft}. PEFT augments this family with an optimistic future-cost table \citep{arabnejad2014peft}, while lookahead HEFT explicitly evaluates a placement's effect on child tasks \citep{bittencourt2010lookahead}. Anticipatory data-movement work similarly argues that future computation and I/O flows should inform movement decisions \citep{bleuse2018anticipation}. SALC is closest in spirit to one-hop lookahead, but its present certificate is narrower: one graph-visible fixed-device successor, runtime residency, and a HEFT-relative damage bound. Prefix-safe scheduling makes no successor prediction at all; it is a bounded online residual around the current HEFT choice.

\textbf{Task runtimes and locality-aware scheduling.} StarPU \citep{augonnet2011starpu}, PaRSEC \citep{bosilca2013parsec}, Legion \citep{bauer2012legion}, and CUDASTF \citep{nvidia2026cudastf} expose different abstractions for task dependencies, placement, and automatic data management on heterogeneous systems. XKaapi's DADA affinity scheduler demonstrated that data-aware placement can substantially reduce transfers without sacrificing much performance \citep{bleuse2014dada}, and later comparisons evaluated HEFT alongside data- and locality-aware multi-CPU/multi-GPU schedulers \citep{lima2015strategies}. Recent work formalizes locality scheduling under GPU-memory constraints \citep{gonthier2023taming} and DARTS uses ready-task and bounded-future information to foster locality in GPU/out-of-core linear algebra \citep{gonthier2025darts}. We therefore do not claim first data-aware scheduling. The narrower contribution is a translation-invariant HEFT-prefix gate, a decision-attributed fixed-successor certificate, and explicit separation of repeated, diagnostic, and invalid evidence.

\textbf{Transfer attribution.} HPCToolkit attributes GPU kernels and copies to heterogeneous calling contexts \citep{zhou2021hpctoolkit}. Our runtime-issued transfer stream serves a different diagnostic purpose: it joins scheduler decisions and logical-data identities to requested peer transfers. It does not replace a profiler and cannot establish physical PCIe transactions, overlap, or link time. The benchmark suite uses CUDASTF task graphs \citep{nvidia2026cudastf}; its sparse-CG path is related to the NAS CG kernel family \citep{bailey1991npb}, while MiniWeather adapts the public regional weather mini-app \citep{norman2020miniweather}.

\section{Conclusion}\label{conclusion}

A current-task EFT optimum can be a poor residency decision when a read--write placement moves the only valid version away from a future consumer. Prefix-safe copy-aware HEFT is a general, benchmark-independent admission rule for this HEFT--MSI conflict: it accepts lower-copy placements only within numerical tolerance of the current modeled-prefix finish bound and does not predict the future graph. In the currently qualified strong-opportunity observations, prefix-safe beats the runtime HEFT baseline in all ten pairs, with a 1.241x geomean over the paired observations and zero modeled prefix damage.

Exact gate removal helps one strong-opportunity instance in every pair, while prefix-safe beats no-prefix on the other in every pair despite no-prefix removing substantially more modeled copy. The result supports a condition-dependent risk control: the method is general, while the existence and profitability of a corrective deviation are properties of the observed task/residency state. A fixed-successor SALC case goes one step closer to the motivating chain: one intervention conditionally eliminates seven attributed peer-copy requests and is followed by 27 downstream placement changes. It certifies the constructed one-hop inequality, not complete chain discovery or end-to-end causality.

Every clean group also misses our predeclared 5\% scheduler-share target, including HEFT, while prefix-safe's absolute scheduler cost is lower than HEFT's. The present contribution is therefore a general enforceable invariant with condition-specific positive evidence and an explicit prototype-level deployment limitation. Broader problem-condition coverage is required to distinguish effective cases, safe no-opportunity cases, and regressions; dynamic-arrival evaluation, stronger repeated locality baselines, additional systems, and lower scheduler cost remain necessary before making a universal performance claim.
